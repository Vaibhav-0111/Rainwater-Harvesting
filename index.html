What is Rainwater Harvesting?

Rainwater harvesting is a method that involves collecting and storing rainwater for various uses. This practice is gaining recognition due to its significant role in addressing water scarcity and environmental concerns. In regions facing increasing urbanization and changing climate patterns, the demand for water is rising. Conventional water sources are often overburdened, leading to shortages.

Rainwater harvesting provides a sustainable solution to this problem. During the monsoon season, rainwater is collected and stored for later use. It can serve a wide range of purposes, from household needs like drinking and cooking to agricultural irrigation. By doing this, we reduce our reliance on groundwater and surface water, helping to conserve these vital resources.

This website is your comprehensive guide to rainwater harvesting, focusing on methods, techniques, we will also provide you the services  and the various advantages it offers . We'll explore how this practice can not only address water scarcity but also promote a more environmentally friendly and cost-effective approach to water management. 


What Are The Different Methods To Collect Rainwater?
Get Started
RAIN BARRELS

This method is the most common and one that many people are familiar with. This involves installing a barrel at a gutter downspout to collect rainwater. The actual barrel may be a recycled barrel or a new commercially available rain barrel.

DRY SYSTEM

This method is a variation of a rain barrel set-up, but it involves a larger storage volume. Essentially, the collection pipe “drys” after each rain event since it empties directly into the top of the tank.

WET SYSTEM

This method involves locating the collection pipes underground in order to connect multiple downspouts from different gutters. The rainwater will fill the underground piping and the water will rise in the vertical pipes until it spills into the tank. The downspouts and underground collection piping must have water-tight connections. The elevation of the tank inlet must be below the lowest gutter on the house.

How Do I Create A Complete Rainwater Collection System?

The image below shows a complete rainwater collection system. While some of the components shown are absolutely necessary, not all of the components listed are required. Although, all of these components will help create a harvesting system that is highly functional .

->1)It all starts with your ROOF SURFACE. Nearly all roof surfaces are fine for rainwater harvesting.
->2)Your gutters should have some sort of GUTTER PROTECTION SCREENING in order to keep large debris from entering the gutters.
->3)You can collect rainwater from any type and shape of GUTTER. You do not need a special gutter for harvesting rainwater.
->4)An additional filtration opportunity is with the installation of a RAIN HEAD (downspout filter) that allows for a bit of self-cleaning filtration options.
->5)A FIRST-FLUSH DIVERTER helps to prevent the first flush of contaminated rainwater from entering the tank.
->6)Another rainwater filtration opportunity is with a TANK SCREEN that is installed on the tank entry point. It also helps to keep mosquitoes and pests out.
->7)RAINWATER TANKS come in all sorts of sizes and materials. Click to learn more about all the various types of rainwater harvesting tanks available on the market.
->8)Install an INSECT PROOF FLAP VALVE on the end of the overflow pipe to keep mosquitoes and pests out.
->9)An AUTO-FILL SYSTEM can be installed to keep a minimum amount of water in the tank at all times. This is very important for rainwater tanks that are connected to automatic irrigation systems in order to prevent the pump from running dry.
->10)Select a PUMP SYSTEM to provide pressurized rainwater to distribute the rainwater easier or to connect to an inground irrigation system.
->11)Install an IRRIGATION FILTER inline after the pump in order to catch any large debris that may have gotten through the pump.
->12)A WATER LEVEL INDICATOR can be helpful with monitoring the water usage from the tank. Those are available in simple gauge tank options along with wireless digital options.

Is Rainwater Harvesting safe?

Rainwater can carry bacteria, parasites, viruses, and chemicals that could make you sick, and it has been linked to disease outbreaks. The risk of getting sick from rainwater may be different depending on your location, how frequently it rains, the season, and how you collect and store the rainwater.

What is the self-life of Rainwater Harvesting?

Rainwater itself does not have a specific shelf life in the same way perishable goods do. However, the stored rainwater's quality can deteriorate over time due to various factors, such as contamination, the growth of bacteria or algae, and environmental pollutants. Therefore, the usability and quality of harvested rainwater depend on how it is collected, stored, and maintained

How much water can be saved?

The amount of water that can be saved through rainwater harvesting depends on several factors, including the size of the catchment area (such as the roof), the local rainfall patterns, the efficiency of the harvesting system, and the intended uses of the harvested water. Here are some key points to consider:

->Catchment Area: The larger the catchment area (such as the roof of a building), the more rainwater can be collected. A larger roof can capture more rainwater during each rainfall event.

->Rainfall Patterns: The amount of rainfall in a specific region and its frequency play a significant role. Areas with regular and abundant rainfall will have more opportunities to collect rainwater compared to regions with sporadic or low rainfall.

->Efficiency of the System: The efficiency of the rainwater harvesting system, including the design of gutters, downspouts, filters, and storage tanks, determines how much of the available rainwater can be effectively captured and stored.

->Intended Uses: The intended uses of harvested rainwater also affect how much water can be saved. Rainwater can be used for various non-potable purposes, such as irrigation, flushing toilets, washing clothes, and cleaning. If the harvested water is used for these purposes instead of using treated, potable water, significant water savings can be achieved.

->Calculations: Engineers and environmental specialists can conduct calculations based on the catchment area and average rainfall data to estimate the potential amount of water that can be harvested in a specific location. These calculations can provide a rough idea of the potential water savings
